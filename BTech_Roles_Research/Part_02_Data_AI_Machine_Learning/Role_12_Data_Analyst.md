## Required Skills

### Core Technical Skills:

**SQL (MOST IMPORTANT):**
- **Critical competency:** 70% of DA work is SQL
- Complex queries: Joins, aggregations, subqueries
- Window functions, CTEs (Common Table Expressions)
- Query optimization basics
- Database: PostgreSQL, MySQL, SQL Server
- **Mastery level required:** This is your bread and butter

**Excel (Essential):**
- Advanced formulas: VLOOKUP, INDEX-MATCH, SUMIFS
- Pivot tables and pivot charts
- Data validation and conditional formatting
- Basic VBA/macros (beneficial but not always required)
- Large dataset handling

**Programming (Important but secondary to SQL):**
- **Python:** Pandas, NumPy, Matplotlib (increasingly important)
- **R:** For statistical analysis (less common, optional)
- Basic scripting for automation

### Data Visualization & BI Tools:

**Business Intelligence Platforms:**
- **Tableau** (most popular in India)
- **Power BI** (Microsoft ecosystem, growing fast)
- **Looker** (used in tech companies)
- **Metabase, Superset** (open-source)
- **Google Data Studio** (free, good for startups)

**Visualization:**
- Chart selection based on data type
- Dashboard design principles
- Storytelling with data
- Color theory and accessibility

### Statistical Knowledge:

**Basic Statistics:**
- Descriptive statistics: Mean, median, mode, standard deviation
- Distributions and probability
- Correlation and causation
- Hypothesis testing basics
- A/B testing fundamentals
- Statistical significance

**Not Required (Unlike DS):**
- Advanced ML algorithms
- Deep statistical modeling
- Complex mathematical proofs

### Business & Domain Skills:

**Business Acumen:**
- Understanding business metrics (KPIs)
- Revenue, growth, churn, retention metrics
- Funnel analysis
- Cohort analysis
- RFM analysis (Recency, Frequency, Monetary)

**Communication:**
- Presenting insights to stakeholders
- Creating executive summaries
- Translating technical findings to business language
- Report writing

**Domain Knowledge:**
- Industry-specific metrics (e-commerce, finance, SaaS, etc.)
- Understanding business context
- Customer behavior analysis

### Additional Skills (Beneficial):

- Google Analytics (web analytics)
- Basic ETL concepts
- Data cleaning and preprocessing
- Cloud platforms basics (AWS, GCP)
- Version control: Git (basic usage)

## Day-to-Day Work

A typical day for a Data Analyst in India involves:

- **SQL Queries & Data Extraction (40-50%):**
  - Writing SQL queries to extract data from databases
  - Joining multiple tables to create analysis datasets
  - Data validation and quality checks
  - Ad-hoc data requests from stakeholders

- **Data Analysis & Exploration (20-30%):**
  - Exploratory data analysis (EDA)
  - Identifying trends, patterns, anomalies
  - Performing cohort analysis, funnel analysis
  - Statistical analysis (A/B test evaluation, correlation)

- **Visualization & Dashboarding (15-20%):**
  - Creating dashboards in Tableau/Power BI
  - Designing charts and visualizations
  - Updating existing reports
  - Building automated reporting systems

- **Meetings & Stakeholder Communication (10-15%):**
  - Presenting findings to business teams
  - Understanding requirements from product/marketing teams
  - Clarifying data definitions and metrics
  - Collaborative problem-solving sessions

- **Data Cleaning & Preparation (10-15%):**
  - Handling missing values, outliers
  - Data transformation and normalization
  - Creating derived metrics
  - Excel data manipulation

- **Documentation (5%):**
  - Documenting analysis methodology
  - Creating data dictionaries
  - Writing analysis reports

### Typical Projects & Tasks:

**Reporting & Dashboards:**
- Sales performance dashboards
- Marketing campaign effectiveness reports
- Customer behavior analytics
- Product usage metrics
- Financial reporting and MIS

**Analysis:**
- Customer segmentation analysis
- Churn analysis and retention metrics
- Pricing analysis and optimization
- Inventory analysis
- User journey and funnel analysis
- A/B test evaluation

**Insights & Recommendations:**
- Identifying revenue opportunities
- Cost optimization analysis

- Anomaly detection and investigation

## Growth Potential

### Career Progression Path:

**Entry Level (0-2 years):** Junior/Associate Data Analyst → Data Analyst
- Focus: Learning SQL, building reports, understanding business metrics
- Deliverables: Regular reports, ad-hoc analysis, dashboard maintenance

**Mid Level (3-6 years):** Senior Data Analyst → Lead Data Analyst
- Focus: Complex analysis, independent project ownership, mentoring
- Deliverables: Strategic insights, advanced analytics, process improvements

**Senior Level (7-10 years):** Principal Analyst → Analytics Manager
- Focus: Team management, strategy, cross-functional leadership
- Deliverables: Analytics roadmap, team development, high-impact projects

**Leadership Track:**
- Lead Analyst → Analytics Manager → Senior Manager → Director of Analytics → VP of Analytics/Insights

**Specialist Track:**
- Senior Analyst → Principal Analyst → Distinguished Analyst (less common)
- Specialization by domain: Marketing Analytics, Product Analytics, Financial Analytics

**Lateral/Upward Moves:**
- **Data Scientist** (most common transition - learn ML, statistics)
- **Business Analyst** (more product/business focus)
- **Data Engineer** (learn engineering, pipelines)
- **Product Analyst** (product-specific insights)
- **Product Manager** (leveraging analytical skills)
- **Business Intelligence Engineer**

**Timeline:** 4-6 years to reach senior analyst level; many transition to DS/DE within 2-3 years

**Note:** Data Analyst is often a stepping stone to Data Science or Data Engineering. Many use it to enter the data field and then specialize.

## Learning Curve

**Low to Moderate**

### Why Low-Moderate:
- SQL is the primary skill - logical and learnable
- Excel is familiar from school/college
- No advanced mathematics required (unlike DS)
- BI tools are visual and user-friendly
- Business concepts are intuitive
- Entry-level friendly with clear learning path

### Time to Productivity:
- **1-3 months:** Basic queries, simple reports, Excel analysis
- **3-6 months:** Complex SQL, dashboards, independent analysis
- **6-12 months:** Advanced analysis, stakeholder management, insights generation
- **1-2 years:** Strategic thinking, mentoring, process improvements

### Easier If You Have:
- Logical thinking and problem-solving skills
- Attention to detail
- Basic programming knowledge
- Curiosity about data and patterns
- Any prior SQL or Excel experience

### Learning Compared to Other Roles:
- **Easier than:** Data Scientist, ML Engineer, Data Engineer
- **Similar to:** Business Analyst, QA Analyst
- **The MOST accessible data role** for fresh graduates

## Stress Level

**Low to Medium**

### Why Low-Medium:
- **Reasonable deadlines:** Mostly predictable reporting cycles
- **Clear deliverables:** Reports, dashboards have defined scopes
- **Lower technical complexity:** Compared to engineering roles
- **Limited on-call:** Rare for analysts (unlike engineers)
- **Supportive role:** Less direct business pressure than decision-makers

### Stress Factors:
- **Ad-hoc urgent requests:** Stakeholders need data "ASAP"
- **Data quality issues:** Messy data requires extensive cleaning
- **Stakeholder management:** Explaining why data says something different than expected
- **Repetitive work:** Similar reports/analysis can be monotonous
- **Tight deadlines during business cycles:** Month-end, quarter-end reporting

### Mitigating Factors:
- Regular working hours (9-6 in most companies)
- Rare weekend work
- Good work-life balance
- Predictable workload (mostly)
- Collaborative environments
- Learning opportunities

### Stress Varies By:
- **Company type:** Startups (higher stress, fast-paced) vs established (structured)
- **Industry:** Finance (strict deadlines) vs others (moderate)
- **Team size:** Solo analyst (higher stress) vs large team (distributed work)

**Overall:** One of the least stressful roles in tech. Good work-life balance in most companies.

## From Day 1 (College First Year)

### Technical Foundation:

**Year 1-2: Build Foundation (Focus on SQL & Excel)**

1. **SQL - YOUR MOST IMPORTANT SKILL:**
   - **Start immediately** - this is 70% of the job
   - Resources:
     - **SQLBolt** (free, interactive - EXCELLENT for beginners)
     - **Mode Analytics SQL Tutorial** (free, practical)
     - **HackerRank SQL** (practice problems)
     - **LeetCode SQL** (interview prep)
   - Practice daily:
     - Install PostgreSQL or MySQL locally
     - Work with sample databases (Sakila, Northwind)
     - Write 100+ queries before graduation
   - Master:
     - SELECT, WHERE, GROUP BY, ORDER BY
     - JOINs (inner, left, right, full)
     - Aggregations (SUM, AVG, COUNT)
     - Window functions (ROW_NUMBER, RANK, LAG, LEAD)
     - Subqueries and CTEs

2. **Excel - Still Very Important:**
   - Advanced Excel course (Udemy, Coursera)
   - Practice:
     - Pivot tables, charts
     - VLOOKUP, INDEX-MATCH
     - IF statements, SUMIFS, COUNTIFS
     - Data cleaning and transformation
   - Work with real datasets from Kaggle

3. **Statistics Basics:**
   - Khan Academy Statistics (free)
   - Understand:
     - Mean, median, mode, standard deviation
     - Distributions (normal, binomial)
     - Correlation vs causation
     - Basic probability
   - No need for advanced math

4. **Programming (Python - Optional but Recommended):**
   - Learn Python basics
   - Focus on: Pandas, NumPy, Matplotlib
   - Not mandatory initially, but helps long-term

**Year 2-3: Visualization & Business Skills**

1. **Tableau/Power BI (Choose One):**
   - **Tableau Public** (free version)
     - Tableau Public Gallery for inspiration
     - Create 5-7 dashboards

     - Create sample dashboards

2. **Statistics & Analytics:**
   - Google Analytics (free certification)

   - Funnel analysis, cohort analysis
   - Metrics and KPI understanding

3. **Domain Knowledge:**

4. **Projects (Very Important):**
   - **SQL Projects:**

     - Financial modeling
     - MIS reports

**Year 3-4: Advanced Skills & Portfolio Building**

## Key Differentiators: Data Analyst vs Similar Roles

### Data Analyst vs Data Scientist:
- **DA:** Descriptive analytics, reporting, SQL-heavy, business insights, lower complexity
- **DS:** Predictive modeling, ML algorithms, Python-heavy, statistical modeling, higher complexity
- **Education:** DA can be BTech/any degree; DS often prefers advanced stats background
- **Transition:** Many DAs upskill to become DS (common career path)

### Data Analyst vs Business Analyst:
- **DA:** Data-focused, SQL/Excel/BI tools, quantitative, dashboard creation
- **BA:** Process-focused, requirements gathering, stakeholder management, documentation
- **Overlap:** Both work with business teams; BA more product/process, DA more data/metrics
- **Tools:** DA uses SQL/Tableau; BA uses JIRA, Confluence, wireframing tools

### Data Analyst vs Data Engineer:
- **DA:** Analyzing data, creating reports, SQL queries, visualization
- **DE:** Building data infrastructure, pipelines, ETL, engineering-heavy
- **Complexity:** DE requires more technical depth (Spark, Airflow, cloud)
- **Transition:** DA can become DE by learning engineering skills

### Data Analyst vs Product Analyst:
- **Product Analyst** is specialized DA focusing on product metrics, user behavior, A/B testing
- Product Analyst often works closely with PM, product teams
- Otherwise similar skill sets
