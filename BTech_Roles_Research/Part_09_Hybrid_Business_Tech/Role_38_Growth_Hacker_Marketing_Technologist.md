## Day-to-Day Work

**Data Analysis & Reporting (30-40%):**
- Analyzing user acquisition funnels (where users drop off)
- Cohort analysis (retention by signup date, channel)
- Building dashboards in Mixpanel/Amplitude/Looker
- SQL queries to extract user data from production database
- Weekly/monthly growth reports (new users, DAU/MAU, churn)
- Attribution analysis (which channels drive best users?)
- LTV (Lifetime Value) and CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) analysis

**Experimentation & A/B Testing (25-35%):**
- Designing A/B tests (hypothesis, variants, success metrics)
- Implementing tests using Optimizely, VWO, or internal tools
- Running experiments (landing pages, email subject lines, onboarding flows)
- Analyzing results (statistical significance, impact)
- Documenting learnings and iterating
- Coordinating with engineering for feature experiments

**Campaign Execution (20-30%):**
- Running paid ad campaigns (Google Ads, Facebook Ads)
- Creating email marketing campaigns
- Launching referral programs
- Content creation for blogs, social media
- Influencer/partnership outreach
- App store optimization (ASO) for mobile apps
- SEO optimization (keyword research, content strategy)

**Growth Strategy & Ideation (10-15%):**
- Brainstorming growth experiments (weekly growth meetings)
- Researching competitor strategies
- Identifying new growth channels to test
- Prioritizing experiments (ICE framework: Impact, Confidence, Ease)
- Building growth roadmap (quarterly OKRs)

**Technical Implementation (10-20%):**
- Setting up tracking (Google Tag Manager, analytics events)
- Building simple landing pages (Webflow, HTML/CSS)
- Writing scripts for data extraction and automation (Python)
- Implementing referral program logic (working with engineers)
- Marketing automation workflows (Zapier, Make)

**Collaboration:**
- Daily standups with growth team
- Weekly sync with product team (feature requests for growth)
- Bi-weekly with engineering (technical requirements)
- Monthly business reviews with leadership (growth metrics)

**Typical Week Example:**
- **Monday**: Analyze weekend performance, plan week's experiments
- **Tuesday**: Launch A/B test on signup flow, optimize Facebook ads
- **Wednesday**: Deep-dive SQL analysis on user retention
- **Thursday**: Growth meeting (present findings, brainstorm new ideas)
- **Friday**: Build dashboard for new metric, write growth blog post

## Stress Level

**Medium**

**Stress Factors:**

1. **Metric Pressure**:
   - Growth targets (20% MoM, 3x YoY, etc.)
   - Visible metrics (everyone sees user growth, revenue)
   - Quarterly OKRs and pressure to hit numbers

2. **Attribution Challenges**:
   - Hard to prove direct impact (was it your campaign or organic?)
   - Failed experiments common (90% of tests fail or are inconclusive)
   - Marketing spend accountability (budget pressure)

3. **Fast-Paced Environment**:
   - Startups move fast, constantly changing priorities
   - Need to adapt quickly to new channels, tools
   - Competitive landscape changes rapidly

4. **Cross-Functional Dependencies**:
   - Need engineering help for implementations (can be bottleneck)
   - Product team may not prioritize growth requests
   - Design bandwidth for creatives

5. **Always-On Campaigns**:
   - Ads run 24/7, need to monitor performance
   - Some weekend/evening work during major campaigns
   - Launch schedules can be intense

**Lower Stress Factors:**

1. **No Production Outages**: Not responsible for uptime, bugs
2. **Creative Outlet**: Experimentation and creativity are fun
3. **Visible Impact**: See your work translate to user growth directly
4. **Variety**: Every day/week is different (not monotonous)
5. **Learning**: Constantly learning new channels, tools, tactics

**Compared to Software Development:**
- **Less on-call stress** (no 2 AM pages)
- **More metric/target pressure**
- **More creative freedom**
- **More ambiguity** (growth is less deterministic than code)

## Personality Fit

### You'll Thrive If:

**Analytical + Creative:**
- You love **data and numbers** (analyzing user behavior, metrics)
- BUT you're also **creative** and can think of unconventional growth ideas
- You can balance left-brain (analysis) and right-brain (creativity)

**Curious & Experimental:**
- You love running experiments and testing hypotheses
- You're comfortable with failure (most growth experiments fail)
- You ask "why?" constantly (why did users drop off? why did this work?)
- You're always learning new tools, channels, tactics

**Hustler Mentality:**
- You're **scrappy** and resourceful (do more with less)
- You don't wait for perfect conditions, you iterate quickly
- You're comfortable wearing multiple hats
- You can get things done without large budgets or teams

**Impact-Driven:**
- You want to see **direct impact** of your work (user growth, revenue)
- You're motivated by metrics and hitting targets
- You enjoy the dopamine hit of a successful campaign
- You want your work to visibly move the needle

**Collaborative:**
- You enjoy working across teams (product, engineering, design, sales)
- You're a good communicator (presenting insights, selling ideas)
- You can influence without authority (convince eng to prioritize your requests)

**Tech-Savvy Marketer:**
- You're technical enough to implement tracking, analyze data, automate
- BUT you also understand marketing psychology, copywriting, design
- You're comfortable with code but also with creative work

### Avoid If:

**Pure Technical Focus:**
- If you want to code all day, choose software engineering
- Growth involves 40% analysis, 30% marketing execution, 30% strategy (limited coding)

**Need Certainty & Predictability:**
- Growth is highly uncertain (90% of experiments fail or are neutral)
- Can be demoralizing if you can't handle failed experiments
- Results not guaranteed despite hard work

**Dislike Marketing/Business:**
- If you find marketing "sleazy" or uninteresting
- If you prefer pure technical problems over business problems
- If you don't care about user acquisition, retention, revenue

**Strongly Introverted:**
- Significant collaboration required (meetings with multiple teams)
- Need to present findings and recommendations regularly
- Customer research involves talking to users

**Impatient:**
- Growth takes time (compounding effects)
- Experiments take weeks to reach statistical significance
- Need patience and persistence

**Dislike Ambiguity:**
- Growth is less structured than engineering
- No "right" answer often (need to test and learn)
- Constantly changing landscape (new channels, tools)

- **Preparation**:
  - Practice SQL queries for marketing analytics
  - Learn growth frameworks (AARRR, ICE prioritization)
  - Study the product thoroughly before assignment

**Round 2: Analytics/Technical Round**
- **SQL Questions**:
  - "Calculate monthly retention cohorts"
  - "Find CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) by channel"
  - "Identify top 10 users by activity"
- **Tools Questions**:
  - "Explain how you would set up tracking for a new feature"
  - "Walk me through a GA4 funnel report"
  - "How do you measure statistical significance in A/B tests?"
- **Metrics Questions**:
  - "What is DAU/MAU ratio and why does it matter?"
  - "How would you calculate LTV for a subscription product?"
  - "Explain the viral coefficient"

**Round 3: Growth Strategy / Case Study**
- **Sample Questions**:
  - "Our retention dropped from 40% to 30%. How would you diagnose and fix it?"
  - "We have â‚¹10 lakh budget to acquire users. How would you spend it?"
  - "Design a referral program for [product]"
  - "How would you improve organic traffic to our blog by 3x?"

- **Framework to Use**:
  1. **Clarify**: Ask questions (what's the product? who's the user? what's been tried?)
  2. **Structure**: Break down problem (Acquisition? Activation? Retention?)
  3. **Hypothesize**: What might be causing the issue?
  4. **Propose Solutions**: 3-5 concrete ideas
  5. **Prioritize**: ICE framework (Impact, Confidence, Ease)
  6. **Measure**: How you'd measure success

**Round 4: Product/Domain Round**
- Deep-dive into the company's product
- "What do you think of our product?"
- "Who are our competitors?"
- "What would you improve about our growth strategy?"
- Preparation: Use the product extensively, research competitors

**Round 5: Behavioral/Culture Fit**
- "Why growth hacking and not software engineering?"
- "Tell me about a growth experiment you ran"
- "Describe a time you failed at something"
- "How do you stay updated with growth trends?"
- STAR method preparation

## Why Choose Growth Hacking?

### Advantages Over Pure Development:

1. **Visible Impact**: See your work translate to user growth, revenue immediately
2. **Creative + Analytical**: Perfect blend of left-brain and right-brain work
3. **Entrepreneurial Skills**: Learn skills critical for starting your own company
4. **Faster Career Growth**: Can become Growth Lead in 5-7 years (vs 7-10 for Engineering Manager)
5. **Variety**: Every day is different (analysis, campaigns, strategy, experiments)
6. **Business Exposure**: Understand revenue, P&L, unit economics
7. **Transferable Skills**: Growth skills apply to any startup/business
8. **High Demand**: Every consumer startup needs growth
9. **Equity Upside**: Joining early-stage startups, ESOPs can be very valuable
10. **Less Coding**: If you like technical work but not coding all day

### Trade-offs:

1. **Metric Pressure**: Constant pressure to hit growth targets
2. **Uncertain Results**: 90% of growth experiments fail or are neutral (can be demoralizing)
3. **Less Technical Depth**: Won't become a deep technical expert
4. **Competitive Field**: Many people want to do growth (from marketing and tech backgrounds)
5. **Startup Dependency**: Role mostly exists in startups (less in traditional companies)
6. **Volatile**: Growth roles can be cut during downturns (happened in 2022-23)
7. **Work-Life Balance**: Can be intense during campaigns, launches

### Ideal Candidate Profile:

- **B.Tech CS student** who loves both technical work AND creative/business thinking
- Strong **analytical skills** (loves data, metrics, insights)
- Creative and **experimental** mindset (comfortable with failure)
- **Hustler mentality** (scrappy, resourceful, gets things done)
- Interested in **consumer products** and understanding user behavior
- Wants to see **direct impact** (not multi-year engineering projects)
- Comfortable with **ambiguity** and fast-paced environments
- Potentially interested in **entrepreneurship** (growth skills invaluable)

**"Should I become a Growth Hacker?"**

**Choose Growth If:**
- You love data but also enjoy creative thinking
- You want to see direct impact of your work on user growth
- You're interested in startups and consumer products
- You want entrepreneurial skills (for future startup)
- You enjoy experimenting and iterating quickly
- You're comfortable with marketing and business concepts

**Stay in Pure Engineering If:**
- You love coding and want to build products (not just grow them)
- You prefer predictable, deterministic work (code works or doesn't)
- You want deep technical expertise in one area
- You dislike marketing/sales/business side
- You need certainty and stable, structured environment
